# Congressional Summary - 2026-03-19

[https://www.congress.gov/congressional-record/volume-172/issue-48/daily-digest](https://www.congress.gov/congressional-record/volume-172/issue-48/daily-digest)

Congress began consideration of the Save America Act (S. 1383) on March 17, 2026, focusing on a House amendment to establish a Veterans Advisory Committee on Equal Access. The Senate agreed to proceed to legislative session and laid the House message before the full chamber by a 51-48 vote, with continued consideration scheduled for March 18 at noon. Additionally, the Senate confirmed Anna St. John as United States District Judge for the Eastern District of Louisiana by a 51-45 vote, and appointed General John M. "Jack" Keane to the Commission on the National Defense Strategy. Twenty-three bills and nine resolutions were introduced during the session.

The House passed two suspension measures on March 16-17: the Mitigation Action and Watermen Support Act (H.R. 4294) by a 320-66 vote, directing the Secretary of Commerce to establish a blue catfish pilot program in the Chesapeake Bay Watershed, and the Small Business Innovation and Economic Security Act (S. 3971) by a 345-41 vote, extending the SBIR and STTR federal research grant programs. The House also adopted House Resolution 1115 by a 196-193 vote to establish procedures for considering three bills: H.R. 556 on lead ammunition restrictions, H.R. 1958 on deporting aliens convicted of fraud, and H.R. 4638 on harming law enforcement animals, along with extended debate time for a constitutional amendment on balanced budgets.

Section 2 of this Congressional Record encompasses remarks and legislative activity from March 17, 2026, reflecting diverse policy concerns across both chambers. The House morning session included brief one-minute recognitions honoring local institutions and individuals, such as MyHometownBronxville news outlet and Christopher Meriwether Demm's retirement from radio broadcasting, alongside substantive policy advocacy addressing wealth inequality, hunger reduction, and military service recognition. Representative Goldman introduced the ROBINHOOD Act to tax unrealized gains on billionaire stock holdings, arguing the current system allows the wealthy to pay far lower effective tax rates than working Americans, while Representative McGovern highlighted the importance of nutrition education programs and criticized cuts to SNAP assistance. The Senate engaged in extended debate on the SAVE America Act regarding voter identification requirements, with Senator Lee arguing the legislation strikes a necessary balance between making voting accessible and preventing election fraud through citizenship verification, while Senator Cantwell opposed the measure as voter suppression that would disenfranchise millions despite evidence showing very low rates of actual noncitizen voting. Senator Schiff broadly critiqued the administration's Iran policy and economic failures, contending the SAVE Act represents a cynical attempt to restrict voting in response to unpopular policies rather than address genuine governance challenges.

Amendment 4447 from Senator Blunt Rochester addresses healthcare affordability and patient protections under the Affordable Care Act, extending enhanced premium tax credits through 2028 and establishing stronger oversight of marketplace operations. The amendment restores the Navigator program with $100 million in federal funding for fiscal year 2026, requires health insurers to notify current enrollees of eligibility changes within 15 days, and implements penalties of up to $1,000 per person per day for non-compliance with notification requirements. Additionally, it allows automatic reenrollment from bronze to silver level plans and includes protections against surprise premium bills by capping out-of-pocket costs based on income levels, with increases capped at $600 to $2,500 depending on household income.

The amendment further stabilizes insurance markets by preventing dramatic premium increases tied to employer-sponsored insurance and requiring states to accept applications from individuals denied advance premium tax credits during income verification processes. It includes provisions for special monthly enrollment periods for low-income households earning below 150 percent of the poverty line and mandates that health insurers maintain compliance with these requirements through submission of attestations to the Department of Health and Human Services, with detailed reporting to Congressional committees regarding implementation across plan years 2025 and 2026.

Senator Wyden submitted five amendments addressing voting access, privacy protections, and surveillance authority, while Senators Murphy and Whitehouse submitted amendments on gun purchases, tax policy, and campaign finance disclosure. The Vote at Home Act would require states to mail ballots to all registered voters at least two weeks before federal elections and provide free postage for voting by mail, with implementation beginning in 2028. The Privacy Act Modernization Act would expand the Privacy Act's definitions of protected information and strengthen civil remedies for violations, with a two-year implementation period and expedited effective dates for entities involving the Department of Government Efficiency. The Cell-Site Simulator Warrant Act would prohibit use of cell-site simulators except under warrant, with limited exceptions for emergencies, foreign intelligence, research, and correctional facilities, requiring detailed disclosures of potential disruptions and third-party testing certification. The amendments on voter registration through motor vehicle authorities would streamline and automate registration processes while protecting against fraudulent registration, with 180-day implementation timelines. Senator Murphy's amendment would require documentary proof of U.S. citizenship for assault weapon purchases. Senators Whitehouse's amendments address tax policy on foreign corporations, windfall profits on crude oil with consumer rebates, Social Security payroll taxes on higher incomes, and comprehensive campaign finance disclosure requirements for organizations making campaign-related disbursements exceeding $10,000, including beneficial ownership information and donor identification for amounts over specified thresholds.

Senator Whitehouse's amendment SA 4408, titled the "Supreme Court Ethics, Recusal, and Transparency Act of 2026," proposes comprehensive reforms to judicial ethics and accountability at the Supreme Court level. The amendment would establish a mandatory code of conduct for Supreme Court justices within 180 days of enactment, require public disclosure of ethics rules on the Court's website in searchable format, and create a complaint mechanism for alleged ethics violations modeled after existing procedures for federal judges. Additionally, it would establish minimum gift and disclosure standards requiring justices to follow Senate and House rules regarding gift acceptance and disclosure, and expand disqualification requirements to include situations involving lobbying contacts or substantial financial support for a justice's nomination or appointment, as well as financial relationships with parties to cases extending back six years.

The amendment further establishes a process for reviewing certified disqualification motions through randomly selected judicial panels for lower court judges and the full Supreme Court for justice-related motions, requires parties and amici to disclose financial relationships and lobbying activities related to justices, and mandates expanded disclosures from amicus filers identifying significant contributors. It also directs the Supreme Court and Judicial Conference to prescribe rules preventing amicus briefs that would create conflicts of interest, and requires the Federal Judicial Center to conduct biennial studies on compliance with disqualification rules with findings reported to Congress and subject to Government Accountability Office review.

Section 6 of this Congressional Record digest contains four substantial amendments addressing fiscal policy and government operations. Senator Whitehouse submitted three amendments establishing new tax provisions: the Big Oil Windfall Profits Tax Act, which would impose an excise tax on crude oil when prices exceed 2025 baseline levels and fund gasoline rebates to eligible individuals; the Medicare and Social Security Fair Share Act, which would raise the payroll tax wage base to $400,000 and impose additional payroll and net investment income taxes on high earners to shore up Social Security and Medicare trust funds; and the No Tax Breaks for Outsourcing Act, which would modify international tax provisions to discourage corporate inversions and offshore income shifting through changes to foreign tax credits, controlled foreign corporation rules, and related corporate tax structures. Additionally, Senator Murphy submitted a brief amendment requiring documentary proof of citizenship for assault weapon purchases, while Senator Wyden submitted four separate amendments: the Cell-Site Simulator Warrant Act establishing warrant and emergency use requirements for law enforcement deployment of cell-site simulators with oversight provisions; the Privacy Act Modernization Act expanding privacy protections and definitions to cover broader categories of personally identifiable information and increasing civil and criminal penalties for violations; an amendment prohibiting federal funding for Executive Order 14243 and contracts with Palantir Technologies for processing personally identifiable information; and the Vote at Home Act establishing vote-by-mail procedures, free postage for ballots, and streamlined voter registration through motor vehicle authorities. These amendments collectively address tax reform, government surveillance, privacy rights, voting access, and the related infrastructure supporting elections and government operations.

Amendment SA 4420 proposes comprehensive legislation titled the "Safeguard American Voter Eligibility Act" or "SAVE America Act," which substantially modifies voter registration and election procedures for federal office. The amendment establishes stringent documentary proof of citizenship requirements for voter registration, mandating that applicants present specific forms of identification such as passports, military IDs, or certified birth certificates. States must verify citizenship status through the Department of Homeland Security's SAVE system and other data sources, with procedures to remove non-citizens from voter rolls within quarterly timeframes. The amendment also imposes photo identification requirements for in-person voting and establishes limited absentee voting options restricted to narrow categories including military personnel, individuals with medical conditions, primary caregivers, those with verified travel, and hardship cases determined by state officials. Additional provisions create detailed ballot handling, chain-of-custody, and receipt procedures with strict deadlines, require election-specific requests for absentee ballots, and establish private rights of action and enforcement mechanisms including potential loss of federal election funding for non-compliant states.

The amendment incorporates two additional titles addressing athletic participation and pediatric medical procedures. Title II amends Title IX to prohibit individuals whose sex is male from participating in athletic programs designated for women or girls, defining sex based solely on reproductive biology and genetics at birth. Title III creates federal criminal penalties for genital and bodily mutilation and chemical castration of minors, establishing up to 10-year prison sentences and creating civil liability for victims, while including narrow medical exceptions for treating disorders of sexual development and conditions threatening major bodily functions.

Senator Cortez Masto submitted an amendment to S. 3183, a bill addressing wildland firefighter safety standards, that would redirect federal funding from immigration enforcement to law enforcement hiring programs. The amendment would transfer $45 billion in appropriated funds to the Edward Byrne Memorial Justice Assistance Grant Program for fiscal year 2025 and modify existing reconciliation act provisions to establish a COPS hiring program under the Attorney General's jurisdiction. The amendment would also waive certain grant requirements for jurisdictions employing fewer than 175 law enforcement officers, including counties, municipalities, and tribal governments, extending the availability of these funds through September 30, 2030.

The amendment represents a significant reallocation of resources within federal law enforcement priorities, converting immigration-related spending authorized under earlier reconciliation legislation into general law enforcement support. This funding structure and the waiver of standard matching requirements would facilitate broader participation from smaller law enforcement agencies in federal grant programs designed to support community policing initiatives.

The Senate section addresses the SAFEGUARD AMERICAN VOTER ELIGIBILITY ACT (SAVE America Act), with extensive debate over a bill requiring proof of citizenship and photo identification to vote in federal elections. Republicans, led by Senator Thune, argue the measure is necessary to ensure election integrity and prevent noncitizen voting, citing public support for voter ID requirements ranging from 70-83 percent. They contend the bill represents basic common-sense verification without disenfranchising eligible citizens, comparing voting requirements to everyday activities like boarding planes or purchasing alcohol. Democrats, including Senators Schumer, Murray, Klobuchar, and others, characterize the bill as voter suppression that would disenfranchise millions of Americans, particularly married women whose names differ from their birth certificates, rural voters, students, seniors, military personnel, and low-income individuals. They argue the requirement for specific documents like passports (costing $165 and unavailable to half of Americans) and the elimination of online and mail-in registration would create insurmountable barriers, while provisions mandating state voter roll submission to the Department of Homeland Security risk erroneous purges. Democrats emphasize that voter fraud is virtually nonexistent and accuse Republicans of exploiting unfounded election integrity concerns to suppress votes ahead of the 2026 midterms, citing President Trump's own statements that passage would guarantee Republican electoral success for decades.

The Senate convened to debate and advance the SAVE America Act, a Republican-backed package that would require proof of citizenship to register to vote and photo identification at polling places, while also addressing transgender medical procedures for minors and women's sports eligibility. Republicans argued the measure represents common-sense election integrity protections supported by over 80 percent of Americans, citing concerns about noncitizens voting and pointing to successful implementation in 36 states already having voter ID laws. Democrats strongly opposed the bill as voter suppression disguised as election security, contending it would result in millions of citizens being wrongly removed from voter rolls through a flawed algorithm designed by Elon Musk's DOGE, would eliminate accessible registration methods like online and mail registration, and would impose onerous documentation requirements that most Americans cannot easily meet. The Senate also advanced a cloture motion on the nomination of Anna St. John to be United States District Judge for the Eastern District of Louisiana, with the motion passing 48-45 along largely party lines, and addressed other matters including Nebraska's unprecedented wildfire disaster affecting nearly 750,000 acres with one fatality, Democratic criticism of Trump administration policies regarding Iran and energy costs, and ongoing disputes over Department of Homeland Security funding as a shutdown continued into its thirty-second day.
