# Congressional Summary (run on 2026-05-02)

[https://www.congress.gov/congressional-record/volume-172/issue-76/daily-digest](https://www.congress.gov/congressional-record/volume-172/issue-76/daily-digest)

On April 30, 2026, Congress continued significant legislative activity across both chambers, with the House passing the Farm, Food, and National Security Act of 2026 by a vote of 224-200 to advance agricultural policy reforms through fiscal year 2031, while also extending Foreign Intelligence Surveillance Act authorities by a vote of 261-111. The Senate focused on Federal Reserve Board nominations, including Kevin Warsh's consideration for both board member and chairman positions, while committees held appropriations hearings on the Air Force, Space Force, and Indian Health Service budgets.

The House's FISA extension represented a 43-day compromise extending the repeal date from April 30 to June 12, 2026, reflecting deep divisions between lawmakers prioritizing national security and those demanding stronger constitutional protections. Supporters, including House Intelligence Committee leadership, argued the program is essential for foreign intelligence operations, while critics from both parties pointed to documented cases of abuse targeting journalists, Members of Congress, and political activists without adequate oversight, calling for judicial warrants before surveilling American citizens.

The Farm, Food, and National Security Act underwent extensive debate with numerous amendments addressing agricultural issues, including provisions for honey testing standards, prohibiting foreign adversaries from purchasing American agricultural land, and exempting farm equipment from Clean Air Act emission standards. The farm equipment amendment generated particular controversy, with supporters arguing Clean Air Act standards harm family farmers through increased costs while opponents questioned whether such exemptions addressed farmers' actual concerns about market access and trade policy.

Two new legislative initiatives were introduced including Senator Thune's Accelerating Broadband Permits Act of 2026, which aims to improve implementation of the Broadband Equity, Access, and Deployment Program through better tracking and addressing federal permitting delays. Senator Durbin introduced the Strengthening Educator Workforce Data Act with cosponsors Senators Hirono and Booker, mandating enhanced federal data collection on teacher and principal employment, experience, certification, and subject-matter expertise, disaggregated by race, ethnicity, and sex while maintaining privacy protections.
