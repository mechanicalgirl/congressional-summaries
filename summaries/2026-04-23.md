# Congressional Summary (run on 2026-04-23)

[https://www.congress.gov/congressional-record/volume-172/issue-70/daily-digest](https://www.congress.gov/congressional-record/volume-172/issue-70/daily-digest)

Congress continued consideration of budget and infrastructure measures on April 21, 2026, as the Senate advanced the fiscal year 2027 budget resolution with remaining debate time equally divided between parties. The Senate passed two bills enhancing background check capabilities for vulnerable populations and addressing conflicts of interest for government employees, while the House passed four bills under suspension including measures on FCC reporting, women's lung cancer research, rural healthcare reauthorization, and hydropower project timelines. Both chambers received numerous nominations across multiple agencies and departments.

Democratic members used a Special Order hour to criticize President Trump's 2027 budget request, arguing it prioritizes military spending at $1.5 trillion while cutting essential social programs including affordable housing, heating assistance, food programs, healthcare, and education. Representatives from the Congressional Progressive Caucus contended that the budget represents inverted priorities, claiming it asks struggling Americans to sacrifice basic necessities while the wealthiest nation on Earth can afford both fiscal responsibility and essential investments in people's fundamental needs.

The House considered the Fire Improvement and Reforming Exceptional Events Act, which amends the Clean Air Act to prevent states from being penalized for emissions from prescribed burns and wildfire mitigation efforts. Supporters argued that prescribed burns produce fewer emissions than uncontrolled wildfires and that current regulations discourage necessary fire management practices. Opponents contended the bill expands the definition of "exceptional events" too broadly to include regular weather conditions like drought, potentially allowing states to exclude pollution from air quality records and undermining public health protections established over fifty years of the Clean Air Act.

Senator Graham's concurrent resolution establishes the congressional budget for fiscal year 2026 through 2035, projecting federal revenues growing from approximately $4.2 trillion to $6.1 trillion while authorizing total outlays rising from $5.5 trillion to $6.7 trillion. The resolution includes reconciliation instructions allowing the Homeland Security and Judiciary Committees to increase deficits by up to $70 billion each through 2035, primarily to fund immigration enforcement operations, and extends Senate budgetary enforcement mechanisms while establishing procedures for emergency appropriations.
