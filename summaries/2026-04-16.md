# Congressional Summary (run on 2026-04-16)

[https://www.congress.gov/congressional-record/volume-172/issue-65/daily-digest](https://www.congress.gov/congressional-record/volume-172/issue-65/daily-digest)

The Senate Committee on Commerce, Science, and Transportation approved nine bills addressing various issues including child safety standards, mental health warnings on social media, satellite communications security, aviation security funding, and aviation professional mental health provisions, along with cybersecurity measures for commercial satellites, quantum initiative reauthorization, FTC reporting on fentanyl access, and regional ocean partnerships. Clay Fuller was sworn in as the new House representative for Georgia's 14th Congressional District after winning a special election with 55.9 percent of the vote, bringing his background as a district attorney, JAG officer, and military veteran to serve rural Georgia. The House overwhelmingly passed the Emergency Conservation Program Improvement Act of 2025 with a 395-10 vote, which expands advance payment options for farmers and forestland owners recovering from natural disasters by increasing upfront funding from 25 to 50-75 percent of project costs, addressing critical delays in agricultural recovery following recent devastating storms. The House also passed multiple bills designating postal facilities in honor of military personnel, first responders, and public servants who exemplified service and sacrifice. Senators Collins and Warner introduced bipartisan legislation to enhance retirement security for family caregivers by eliminating income caps on Roth IRA contributions and enabling catch-up contributions for caregivers, addressing the significant economic hardship faced by the estimated 41 million Americans providing unpaid family care, with women comprising 58 percent of full-time caregivers and 64 percent of American women reporting that caregiving duties negatively impacted their retirement savings. Senator Grassley criticized UBS's obstruction of an investigation into Nazi-era accounts held by the bank, noting that UBS provided misleading testimony about withheld documents, actually withholding over 23,000 pages despite claiming fewer than 150, and announcing plans for a follow-up hearing to ensure the bank's compliance with investigating Nazi assets and looting.
