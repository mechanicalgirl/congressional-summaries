# Congressional Summary (run on 2026-04-30)

[https://www.congress.gov/congressional-record/volume-172/issue-74/daily-digest](https://www.congress.gov/congressional-record/volume-172/issue-74/daily-digest)

On April 28, 2026, both chambers of Congress convened to receive an address from King Charles III of the United Kingdom, marking the first visit by a reigning British monarch since Queen Elizabeth II in 1991 and coinciding with the 250th anniversary of American independence. The King's remarks emphasized the deep historical and contemporary bonds between the two nations, highlighting their shared commitment to democracy, security cooperation through NATO, economic partnership valued at over $430 billion in annual trade, and collaborative efforts in emerging technologies. The legislative day included extensive committee activities focused on fiscal year 2027 budget requests and nominations, with the Senate passing several measures including the Protecting Americans from Russian Litigation Act and the Protect Infant Formula from Contamination Act.

Senator Padilla introduced S. 4417 with Senator Schiff as cosponsor, a bill to amend the Pechanga Band of Luiseno Mission Indians Water Rights Settlement Act of 2016. The legislation would expand the agreement's definition of "Reservation" to allow the Tribe to apply water rights to current and future trust lands rather than only lands held in trust at ratification, and would broaden the water quality fund's permitted uses beyond desalination to include any water quality improvement activities, including addressing emerging contaminants such as PFAS. The Pechanga Band, whose ancestors inhabited the Temecula Valley for over 10,000 years before being displaced in 1875, seeks these amendments to better protect long-term water supply and sustainability of the Santa Margarita River Watershed and Wolf Valley Aquifer while preserving tribal sovereignty. Senator Grassley commemorated the centennial of Henry A. Wallace's pioneering work in American agriculture, noting that Wallace's development of hybrid corn revolutionized Iowa farming by enabling corn yields to increase from approximately 20 bushels per acre in the 1920s to over 200 bushels per acre in the present day, establishing Iowa's identity as the "Tall Corn State."
