# Congressional Summary (run on 2026-06-03)

[https://www.congress.gov/congressional-record/volume-172/issue-92/daily-digest](https://www.congress.gov/congressional-record/volume-172/issue-92/daily-digest)

During the second session of the 119th Congress from January through May 2026, the Senate and House enacted 27 public bills into law while processing 405 total measures and confirming 3,305 executive nominations out of 7,700 received. The Senate convened for 422 hours over 82 days and the House for 262 hours over 75 days, producing significant committee work and legislative activity across numerous policy areas including federal communications, digital commodities regulation, and appropriations bills. The chambers also honored former Senator Donald W. Riegle Jr. of Michigan and processed extensive judicial and executive nominations.

In early June 2026, the Senate scheduled confirmation votes for federal judicial nominees including Kathleen S. Lane for Montana's District Court and Jeffrey M. Kuhlman, while the House considered measures under suspension of rules beginning June 2. Multiple committees held hearings on budget requests, pending nominations, and various policy matters ranging from artificial intelligence to healthcare and oversight issues. New public laws signed in late May included legislation establishing a medal of service for law enforcement officers and amendments to Capitol Police retirement provisions.

During a June 1 session, Senate Democrats and Republicans engaged in contentious debates over judicial nominations, border security, economic concerns, and alleged corruption. Senator Durbin opposed Kathleen Lane's judicial nomination, citing the American Bar Association's "not qualified" rating due to her lack of substantial courtroom experience despite nearly nine years since bar admission. Durbin also attacked the proposed $1.8 billion "Anti-Weaponization Fund" as a corrupt slush fund that would benefit January 6 insurrectionists and Trump loyalists while protecting Trump from IRS audits. Senate Republicans defended border security funding and immigration enforcement, arguing that illegal crossings have declined significantly under Trump's policies, while Democrats contended that high inflation and record energy costs strain American families and pledged to block the fund through legislative amendments.
