# Summary of Congressional Digest for March 2, 2026

## Session Overview and Chamber Activities

The Senate convened on March 2, 2026 at 3 p.m. to continue consideration of H.R. 6644, the Housing for the 21st Century Act. During the session, senators engaged in extensive debate regarding military operations against Iran that President Trump initiated over the weekend. The House met briefly in the morning and adjourned at 9:02 a.m. after minimal business. Both chambers will reconvene on Tuesday, March 3, with the Senate resuming debate on the housing bill at 10 a.m. and the House convening at noon for consideration of measures under suspension of rules.

## Iran Military Operations and War Powers Debate

Senators delivered numerous speeches opposing the President's military action against Iran without congressional authorization. Multiple senators, including Kaine, Schumer, Van Hollen, Duckworth, Baldwin, Welch, Kim, Blumenthal, Schatz, Merkley, and Schiff, argued that the Constitution grants Congress—not the President—the power to declare war. They criticized the absence of an imminent threat to justify the operation and expressed concern about repeating mistakes from Iraq and Afghanistan. Four American servicemembers were killed in the initial Iranian response, and the President acknowledged further casualties were likely. A bipartisan War Powers Resolution was introduced to direct cessation of hostilities pending congressional authorization.

## Legislative Action on Housing

Senate Majority Leader Thune advocated for the ROAD to Housing Act (H.R. 6644), describing it as the most significant housing legislation in a decade. The bill aims to increase housing supply and lower costs by removing regulatory barriers, streamlining environmental reviews, and unleashing private sector investment. Key provisions include measures to reduce manufactured housing costs and increase affordable housing development. Thune emphasized the bill addresses rising home prices—median existing single-family homes reaching $412,500, roughly five times median household income—and does not require taxpayer funding.
