# Congressional Summary (run on 2026-03-27)

[https://www.congress.gov/congressional-record/volume-172/issue-56/daily-digest](https://www.congress.gov/congressional-record/volume-172/issue-56/daily-digest)

On March 25, 2026, the Senate continued consideration of the House message on the Save America Act with voting scheduled for March 26, while passing several bills including the Hershel 'Woody' Williams National Medal of Honor Monument Location Act and Golden Gate National Recreation Area boundary adjustments. The House passed the Make the District of Columbia Safe and Beautiful Act of 2026 by a vote of 218-206, establishing a beautification program and federal commission focused on public safety in the nation's capital that codifies elements of President Trump's executive order on D.C. safety. Republicans supported the measure as addressing crime and deteriorating conditions in Washington, while Democrats opposed it as federal overreach into local governance that prioritizes immigration enforcement over genuine community safety for residents who lack full voting representation in Congress.

Congress honored several distinguished individuals during this session, including Sergeant Benjamin N. Pennington, a 26-year-old soldier killed on March 8 during an enemy attack in Saudi Arabia, and Judge Eric F. Melgren of the United States District Court for Kansas, recognized for nearly two decades of judicial service. Representatives also celebrated community leaders and advocates including Reverend Dr. Stephen Pogue of Greater Centennial A.M.E. Zion Church in Mount Vernon, Elizabeth Ramirez who founded Parents for Peace and Justice following her son's fatal shooting, and Dr. Jessica Esquivel, a particle physicist working to expand opportunities for underrepresented communities in science.

Senator Lee delivered an extensive address on the SAVE America Act, which requires voter identification and citizenship verification for federal elections, grounding his arguments in constitutional principles and emphasizing that the legislation represents basic good governance. He noted the bill's approximately 80-85 percent public support across party and demographic lines and refuted opposition claims that the measure disenfranchises women or racial minorities. Lee traced the constitutional history of federalism and separation of powers, explaining how Congress possesses enumerated authority over federal elections and drawing a parallel to the Civil Rights Act of 1964 to suggest that sustained debate could overcome current Senate opposition.

Several amendments to pending legislation addressed diverse policy areas including redistricting reform and tribal voting identification.
