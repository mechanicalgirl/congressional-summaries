# Congressional Summary (run on 2026-03-29)

[https://www.congress.gov/congressional-record/volume-172/issue-58/daily-digest](https://www.congress.gov/congressional-record/volume-172/issue-58/daily-digest)

On March 27, 2026, the House of Representatives passed H.R. 7084, the Defending American Property Abroad Act, by a vote of 247-164, authorizing the President to restrict vessels from entering U.S. ports if they have visited facilities expropriated from American owners by Western Hemisphere governments with free trade agreements with the United States. The legislation directly addresses a dispute between the Mexican government and Alabama-based Vulcan Materials Company over a limestone quarry and deepwater port facility in Mexico's Yucatan Peninsula that the company has operated since the 1980s. Supporters argued the measure protects American investments abroad and enforces consequences for government seizure of U.S. property, while opponents contended the bill diverts Coast Guard resources and inappropriately uses maritime law instead of existing USMCA arbitration mechanisms and diplomatic channels.

The House also voted 213-203 on March 27 to agree to the Senate amendment to H.R. 7147, the Homeland Security and Further Additional Continuing Appropriations Act for 2026, after the Rules Committee approved the measure on March 26. Additionally, the House introduced 27 new public bills and six resolutions and conducted commemorative speeches during Women's History Month. Both chambers are scheduled to reconvene on March 30 for pro forma sessions, with the Senate meeting at 10:30 a.m. and the House at noon.

The House debated and approved a rule for a 60-day continuing resolution to fund the Department of Homeland Security through May 22, 2026, after the Senate passed a competing bipartisan funding bill unanimously. Republicans argued their measure was necessary to fully fund border security operations and ICE without compromise, citing recent deaths caused by illegal immigrants. Democrats opposed the House resolution as partisan obstruction, contending the Senate's bipartisan bill should receive an up-or-down vote and criticized Republicans for refusing to compromise on TSA, FEMA, and Coast Guard funding to expand ICE's already substantial $75 billion budget. The House adopted the rule on a vote of 213-203, moving forward with Speaker Johnson's approach despite Democratic calls for accepting the Senate's compromise.
