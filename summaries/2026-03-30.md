# Congressional Summary (run on 2026-03-30)

[https://www.congress.gov/congressional-record/volume-172/issue-58/daily-digest](https://www.congress.gov/congressional-record/volume-172/issue-58/daily-digest)

During Friday's House session on March 27, the chamber passed the Defending American Property Abroad Act with a vote of 247-164, granting the President authority to restrict vessels from entering U.S. ports if they transit ports in Western Hemisphere countries that have illegally seized American-owned maritime infrastructure. The legislation specifically addresses Mexico's 2022 expropriation of a deepwater port and limestone quarry owned by Alabama-based Vulcan Materials Company on the Yucatan Peninsula. Supporters, including multiple Alabama representatives, argued the measure provides necessary recourse after diplomatic efforts failed, while opponents contended it inappropriately inserts Congress into an international property dispute that should be resolved through trade agreement arbitration mechanisms and places additional enforcement burdens on the Coast Guard.

The House also debated and approved a rule governing consideration of the Department of Homeland Security appropriations bill following a 42-day government shutdown. Republicans, led by Speaker Mike Johnson, pushed for an eight-week continuing resolution that would fully fund all DHS agencies, emphasizing that security cannot be partial and criticizing Democrats for allegedly holding border security hostage. They highlighted recent crimes committed by individuals in the country illegally and argued their proposal ensures full funding for TSA, FEMA, Coast Guard, and immigration enforcement while allowing time for further negotiations. Democrats opposed the rule and urged passage of a Senate bill that had passed unanimously with bipartisan support, arguing the House proposal prioritized unlimited immigration enforcement funding over paying federal workers and characterized Immigration and Customs Enforcement as a rogue agency responsible for civil rights violations. The rule ultimately passed 213-203, and the continuing resolution funds the Department of Homeland Security through May 22, 2026, while the Senate-passed alternative was blocked from receiving a floor vote.
