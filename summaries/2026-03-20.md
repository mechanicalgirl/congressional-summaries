# Congressional Summary (run on 2026-03-20)

[https://www.congress.gov/congressional-record/volume-172/issue-49/daily-digest](https://www.congress.gov/congressional-record/volume-172/issue-49/daily-digest)

On Wednesday, March 18, 2026, Congress continued its legislative agenda with activity in both chambers. The Senate advanced consideration of the House message accompanying the SAVE America Act (S. 1383), with multiple amendments pending as of the end of the day, and scheduled further debate for Thursday at noon. The Senate also rejected a motion to discharge the Committee on Foreign Relations regarding a resolution directing removal of U.S. Armed Forces from Iran, and the Veterans' Affairs Committee favorably reported numerous bills addressing benefits, services, and programs for veterans. The House passed two immigration-related measures: H.R. 556 (Protecting Access for Hunters and Anglers Act) restricting limitations on lead ammunition use on federal lands, and H.R. 1958 (Deporting Fraudsters Act), clarifying that aliens convicted of defrauding the government or unlawfully receiving public benefits are deportable. A constitutional amendment requiring a balanced federal budget fell short of the required two-thirds majority for passage under suspension of the rules. Multiple committees held hearings on diverse topics ranging from worldwide security threats and military posture to healthcare costs and federal spending transparency, while various procedural resolutions and 23 new bills were introduced.

The House debated H.J. Res. 139, a proposed constitutional amendment requiring a balanced federal budget, with members fundamentally disagreeing on both the necessity and consequences of such a measure. Sponsor Andy Biggs of Arizona argued that the nation's $38-39 trillion debt and unsustainable spending trajectory demand a constitutional constraint, noting that Congress lacks the political will to balance budgets voluntarily and that families across America already operate within their means. He emphasized that the amendment includes a five-year implementation period and allows emergency spending with a two-thirds vote, while opposing arguments focused on concerns about fiscal flexibility during crises and the amendment's asymmetrical treatment of taxes versus spending cuts.

Opponents, led by ranking member Jamie Raskin of Maryland, characterized the measure as a distraction from Republican fiscal irresponsibility, pointing to the Trump administration's spending on an undeclared war in Iran and prior tax cuts that added trillions to the debt. Democrats highlighted that President Clinton balanced the budget without a constitutional amendment, arguing that the problem is political will rather than institutional constraint, and warned that the amendment would force painful program cuts while preserving preferential tax treatment for the wealthy. The House proceeded with consideration of the resolution under suspension of rules, with final voting postponed under House procedures.

H.R. 1958, the Deporting Fraudsters Act of 2026, proposed by Representative Dave Taylor with support from the House Judiciary Committee, would amend the Immigration and Nationality Act to make aliens convicted of or who admit to committing fraud against the United States government or unlawfully receiving public benefits inadmissible and deportable. The bill specifically targets convictions or admissions related to violations of the Supplemental Nutrition Assistance Program, Social Security fraud, identification document fraud, mail fraud, and other federal fraud offenses, while rendering such aliens ineligible for any immigration relief. Proponents argue the legislation closes loopholes in current law that have allowed removal proceedings to languish for years and highlighted cases such as a Minnesota scandal allegedly involving Somali immigrants committing an estimated $9 billion in fraud, as well as instances where courts determined fraud convictions did not automatically trigger deportability.

Opponents contended the bill is redundant and unnecessary since fraud convictions already constitute deportable offenses under current law, either as crimes involving moral turpitude or specifically enumerated aggravated felonies. Critics emphasized the bill's provision allowing deportation based on an alien's admission without requiring a criminal conviction strips due process protections and prevents crime victims from receiving restitution through the criminal justice system. Debate also centered on broader disputes regarding the Trump administration's immigration enforcement actions, with Republicans highlighting fraud losses to American taxpayers and sanctuary jurisdiction policies, while Democrats condemned ICE tactics and characterized the measure as part of a pattern eroding constitutional protections for both immigrants and citizens.

Section 4 of this Congressional Record digest contains eight amendments submitted by Senator Wyden and others addressing diverse policy areas ranging from watershed management to land conservation to prescribed fire management in Oregon. Senator Wyden's amendments establish several new programs including the Watershed Results Act, which authorizes up to $17 million annually through 2031 to fund partnerships between the Department of Interior and eligible entities to implement cost-effective watershed outcomes projects using advance analytics, with a maximum of five projects authorized. Three related amendments designate approximately 59,512 acres as additions to Oregon's Wild Rogue Wilderness and establish 128,034 acres of new recreation areas (Rogue Canyon and Molalla) while withdrawing federal land in Curry and Josephine Counties from mining and mineral leasing activities.

The section also includes the comprehensive National Prescribed Fire Act establishing a multi-year prescribed fire program across federal lands managed by Interior and Agriculture departments, with authorization for $5 billion annually through 2030 dedicated to landscape restoration and infrastructure modernization at national laboratories. Additional provisions address the Malheur County Grazing Management Program, creating a Community Empowerment for the Owyhee group to oversee land health management and ecological restoration projects with $1 million annual authorization through 2036, along with approximately 1.1 million acres of wilderness designations and land conveyances to the Burns Paiute Tribe in Malheur County. These amendments collectively represent significant land management, conservation, and resource allocation initiatives focused primarily on Oregon federal lands and natural resources management.

Section 5 presents a diverse collection of proposed amendments to S. 1383, spanning topics from military benefits to election integrity to child care policy. Several amendments address reproductive and fertility services, with Mrs. Murray proposing comprehensive provisions for both active-duty service members and veterans to access fertility treatment, counseling, and adoption assistance regardless of sex, gender identity, or marital status, alongside establishing procedures for fertility preservation after service-related injuries. Other significant proposals include amendments from Ms. Alsobrooks concerning election security, such as prohibitions on deceptive voter suppression communications and artificial intelligence-generated election misinformation, alongside measures addressing immigration enforcement practices and vaccine policy disputes. Additional amendments by Mr. Blumenthal and Mr. Coons address disability retiree compensation, firearm transfer regulations, and restrictions on immigration enforcement at polling places. Mrs. Murray also submitted an extensive child care and early learning proposal establishing a birth-through-five child care entitlement program with universal preschool services, representing substantial new federal funding and requirements for states to provide affordable, high-quality care with adequate provider compensation and sliding-scale family copayments through fiscal 2032.

Senator Murray's amendment establishes the Wage Theft Prevention and Wage Recovery Act, which aims to strengthen worker protections and increase penalties for wage violations. The amendment requires employers to provide comprehensive wage disclosures within 15 days of hiring, include detailed information on paystubs regarding hours, rates, overtime, and deductions, and deliver final payments within 14 days of employment termination. It significantly increases civil and criminal penalties for wage and hour violations, with civil fines reaching $110,150 per employee for repeated violations and criminal penalties enhanced to $10,000 per employee. The amendment also extends the statute of limitations for wage claims from two to four years for standard claims and three to five years for willful violations, tolls the limitation period during Department of Labor investigations, and establishes a grant program supporting community-based organizations and partnerships to prevent wage violations and assist workers in recovering unpaid wages through outreach, training, and enforcement cooperation.

Section 7 presents a series of amendments submitted by Senator Wyden and others regarding various tax and regulatory matters. The amendments include corporate tax rate increases ranging from 21.1 percent to 22.6 percent, modifications to railroad track maintenance credits with inflation adjustments through 2027, and extensions of clean energy tax credits with modified greenhouse gas emissions thresholds. Additional proposals address tax-free production of low-alcohol kombucha, modifications to the qualified business income deduction with tiered reductions based on income levels, and work incentive reforms for childhood disability beneficiaries. The section also contains comprehensive tax return preparer regulation provisions establishing minimum competency standards including examinations and background checks, amendments to ensure all legally married couples receive equal tax treatment through gender-neutral language updates, housing tariff exclusion provisions to reduce construction costs by allowing duty-free imports of critical building materials, and extensive partnership tax reform provisions addressing basis shifting, distributed property treatment, and net investment income calculations for high-income individuals.

Senator Wyden submitted three significant tax-related amendments addressing small business support and carried interest taxation. The first amendment, titled the "Providing Real Opportunities for Growth to Rising Entrepreneurs for Sustained Success (PROGRESS) Act," establishes two new tax credits for small businesses: a small business investor tax credit allowing investors to claim up to 10 percent of qualified investments in eligible businesses, capped at $10,000 annually, and a first employee business wage credit providing a 25 percent credit on qualified wages paid by qualifying business entities, also capped at $10,000 yearly. Both credits target entities with qualified active investors owning over 50 percent ownership and impose income limitations on individual investors. The second amendment, the "Ending the Carried Interest Loophole Act," fundamentally restructures taxation of partnership interests received for services by treating them as ordinary income based on a formula using invested capital and specified rates, with a rate comprising the first segment rate plus 9 percentage points. The third amendment, the "Protection of Social Security Benefits Restoration Act," prohibits administrative offset of Social Security, railroad retirement, and black lung benefits, nullifying prior authority that allowed such offsets and marking a significant protection for beneficiaries' retirement income from debt collection actions.

Section 9 of this Congressional Record digest contains multiple proposed amendments addressing diverse policy areas. Senator Wyden submitted three amendments focused on tax incentives and partnership reforms. The first establishes a small business investor tax credit allowing investors to claim up to 10 percent of qualifying investments in small businesses, with credits capped at $10,000 annually and $50,000 total, alongside a companion credit for first employees of qualifying businesses providing 25 percent of qualified wages up to $40,000 total. The second amendment targets basis-shifting transactions in partnerships by requiring gain recognition when related parties receive distributions with increased basis, implementing a 40 percent accuracy-related penalty for violations, and establishing exceptions for small businesses meeting gross receipt thresholds. The third amendment comprehensively reforms partnership taxation by requiring consistent percentage allocation methods for covered partners in partnerships with related-party ownership, eliminating time limitations on pre-contribution gain taxation, repealing special liquidation distribution rules, and modifying debt allocation and basis adjustment procedures, with most provisions effective for taxable years beginning after enactment.

Senator Wyden submitted numerous amendments covering diverse policy areas, all ordered to lie on the table. The amendments include comprehensive land management proposals for Oregon and Malheur County, establishing grazing management programs and wilderness designations totaling over 1.1 million acres, along with a community empowerment group to oversee land health initiatives. A major prescribed fire initiative authorizes $17 million annually through 2031 to expand controlled burning practices across federal lands, addressing wildfire risk through increased prescribed fire acreage targets and collaborative programs involving federal, state, tribal, and private stakeholders. Recreation-focused amendments designate approximately 128,000 acres in Oregon as recreation areas with wildfire mitigation requirements and expand the Wild Rogue Wilderness by roughly 59,500 acres. On tax policy, the amendments propose raising the corporate tax rate incrementally from 21 percent to 25 percent, increasing the top individual tax rate to 39.6 percent for those earning above $700 million to $1 billion annually, modifying qualified business income deductions, extending clean energy tax credits through 2034, and updating various tax code provisions for gender-neutral language regarding married couples. Additional amendments address housing affordability through tariff exclusions for building materials, regulate tax return preparers with competency standards, expand child disability benefits under Social Security, and exempt low-alcohol kombucha from federal excise taxes.

Mr. Wyden submitted multiple amendments addressing taxation of high-income individuals, proposing to increase the top individual income tax rate from 37 percent to 39.6 percent for taxable income exceeding various threshold amounts ranging from $600 million down to $10 million, all effective for taxable years beginning after December 31, 2025. Ms. Hassan submitted amendments related to repealing Medicare price negotiation exemptions for orphan drugs, establishing exemptions from balance-of-payments duties for specific agricultural imports, and extending enhanced health care premium tax credits under the Affordable Care Act through various years between 2027 and 2030. Mr. Hickenlooper submitted amendments addressing IRS enforcement funding restoration, energy project permitting, federal workforce impacts, data privacy concerns, wildfire preparedness, public land disposal restrictions, and voting access protections, among other policy areas. Ms. Hirono, Mr. Merkley, and other senators submitted comprehensive amendments on topics including the Equal Rights Amendment, voting rights, contraception access, healthcare competition restrictions, and drug pricing controls. Additionally, amendments addressing wage theft prevention, veteran family health services including fertility treatment and adoption assistance, and restrictions on immigration enforcement detention of pregnant women were submitted for consideration.

Section 12 of the Congressional Record contains a comprehensive child care and early learning initiative submitted as an amendment by Mrs. Murray to S. 1383. The amendment comprises four titles establishing federal programs to expand access to affordable, high-quality child care and preschool services. Title I creates a Birth Through Five Child Care and Early Learning Entitlement Program beginning October 1, 2027, guaranteeing assistance to eligible children under age six whose parents participate in work or education activities, with payments to states covering 90 percent of direct service costs on a sliding fee scale based on family income and requiring states to establish tiered quality systems with adequate provider compensation. Title II establishes Building an Affordable System for Early Education (BASE) Grants providing five-year direct funding to child care providers to support staff wages, program quality improvements, and operational sustainability while requiring providers to accept subsidies regardless of actual enrollment. Title III implements a Universal Preschool program for ages three and four, with the federal government covering 90 percent of costs initially declining to 60 percent by fiscal year 2032, requiring states to develop evidence-based standards equivalent to Head Start performance standards and prioritizing high-need communities. Title IV extends Head Start services to full school days and years, with authorizations for facility improvements and wage enhancements to ensure teaching staff receive compensation equivalent to elementary educators.

Senate Resolution 650 recognizes the heritage, culture, and contributions of approximately 5.3 million American Indian, Alaska Native, and Native Hawaiian women in the United States. The resolution celebrates their achievements across military service, public service, business, education, science, medicine, literature, and the arts, highlighting notable figures such as Maria Tallchief, a prima ballerina honored on the 2023 dollar coin; Wilma Mankiller, the first female Principal Chief of the Cherokee Nation; and Susan La Flesche Picotte, the first Native American to earn a medical degree. The resolution emphasizes their vital roles in advancing civil rights, protecting human rights, advocating for land rights, and safeguarding the environment, while also documenting their economic contributions, with over 11,600 Native women-owned businesses employing more than 72,000 workers and generating over $11.2 billion in revenue as of 2024.

The resolution passed the Senate by unanimous consent and calls on the United States to continue investing in the future of American Indian, Alaska Native, and Native Hawaiian women by addressing barriers they face in access to justice, healthcare, and opportunities for educational and economic advancement. It recognizes these women as "life givers, the culture bearers, and the caretakers of Native peoples" whose contributions have enriched the lives of all Americans, while reaffirming the Senate's commitment to upholding their safety and interests.

Senate Democrats have mounted a fierce opposition to the SAVE America Act, the centerpiece of Republican legislation debate this week, characterizing it as voter suppression disguised as election security. Senator Schumer and others have argued that the bill would disenfranchise millions of American citizens by requiring proof of citizenship through documents like passports or birth certificates, imposing what they describe as a modern poll tax on voters already protected by existing law. They point to minimal documented cases of noncitizen voting—fewer than 100 instances over two decades—and cite real-world examples from New Hampshire's similar law showing eligible voters, including women with name changes and longtime registered voters, being turned away from polls. Meanwhile, Senate Republicans including Senators Cornyn, Schmitt, and Kennedy have defended the bill as commonsense election integrity measures supported by 80 percent of Americans, arguing that requiring citizenship verification and voter identification does not suppress votes but rather prevents cheating. The debate has also encompassed related issues including government funding disputes that have left TSA workers unpaid and questions about the Trump administration's war in Iran, with Democrats criticizing the military expenditures while Republicans defend the necessary defense measures, and broader concerns about administration policies affecting healthcare costs and economic affordability for American families.

Multiple Democratic senators advanced a War Powers Resolution challenging President Trump's military operations in Iran, arguing that the president unilaterally initiated what constitutes the largest military engagement since the Iraq War without congressional authorization or public debate. Senators Booker, Murphy, Kaine, Baldwin, and Duckworth contended that thirteen service members have been killed and over two hundred injured in the conflict, with costs exceeding one billion dollars daily, while emphasizing that the Constitution vests the power to declare war exclusively in Congress, not the executive branch. They criticized the administration's refusal to conduct public hearings or clearly articulate military objectives and an exit strategy, warning that the conflict has already expanded to attacks on eleven countries and disrupted global energy markets with gas prices rising sharply and the Strait of Hormuz blockaded.

Republican senators including Cotton, Graham, and Risch countered that the military action was constitutionally justified under the president's Commander in Chief authority and necessary to prevent Iran from developing nuclear weapons, arguing that Iran has posed an imminent threat to Americans for forty-seven years through proxy attacks and terrorism. They contended that the president had authority to act defensively without prior congressional approval and criticized the War Powers Resolution as constitutionally problematic and operationally dangerous, asserting it would handcuff military objectives before achieving defined goals and abandon allied forces like Israel. Senate Majority Leader Schumer and other Democratic leaders supported the resolution, urging Republican colleagues to fulfill their constitutional duty to debate war and peace decisions rather than acquiesce to executive power expansion.
