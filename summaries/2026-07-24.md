# Congressional Summary (run on 2026-07-24)

[https://www.congress.gov/congressional-record/volume-172/issue-120/daily-digest](https://www.congress.gov/congressional-record/volume-172/issue-120/daily-digest)

The House passed the National Defense Authorization Act for Fiscal Year 2027 on July 22 by a narrow margin of 216-212, along with approving the Stop Insider Trading Act and adopting a concurrent budget resolution for fiscal years 2027-2036. The Senate confirmed two federal judges and continued consideration of additional judicial and executive nominations, with further votes scheduled for Thursday. Both chambers processed various executive communications from federal agencies during this legislative session.

The Stop Insider Trading Act combined congressional stock trading restrictions with voter identification requirements for federal elections. The legislation would ban Members of Congress, their spouses, and dependent children from purchasing individual stocks while imposing penalties of $2,000 or ten percent of transaction value for violations. However, critics noted the bill contained significant loopholes allowing existing stock retention, trading through alternative vehicles like derivatives, and dividend reinvestment. Democrats opposed the measure as insufficient ethics reform and characterized the attached voter identification provisions as a Trojan horse for voter suppression, citing narrow photo ID requirements and an implementation deadline just two weeks before elections.

The concurrent budget resolution establishing federal budgets for fiscal years 2027-2036 became a focal point for partisan debate. Republicans argued the resolution was necessary to provide $60 billion in supplemental military funding for Iran operations, $12 billion in agricultural assistance, and election integrity measures. Democrats countered that the resolution added nearly $100 billion to the deficit to fund what they characterized as an illegal and unpopular war while ignoring affordability crises in healthcare, groceries, housing, and energy. Both sides cited competing budget data, with Republicans claiming $1.6 trillion in spending reductions while Democrats highlighted over $5 trillion added to the national debt in eighteen months.

The House extensively debated numerous amendments to defense authorization legislation covering military chaplaincy protections, security clearance procedures for defense investors, Coast Guard authority over unmanned maritime systems, and comprehensive maritime commerce policies. Significant provisions included establishing minimum inventory requirements for military aircraft and vehicles, creating a Maritime Security Advisor position, and strengthening cargo preference requirements mandating 100 percent U.S.-flag vessel preference for government cargo with phased implementation through 2042.

Democratic senators delivered extended speeches opposing the Trump administration's war with Iran, criticizing both the military campaign's costs and duration and Congress's failure to exercise its constitutional authority over war declarations
