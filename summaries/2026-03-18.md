# Congressional Summary - 2026-03-18

[https://www.congress.gov/congressional-record/volume-172/issue-48/daily-digest](https://www.congress.gov/congressional-record/volume-172/issue-48/daily-digest)

On Tuesday, March 17, 2026, Congress continued its work with the Senate advancing consideration of the Save America Act while the House suspended rules to pass two measures addressing blue catfish sales and small business innovation programs. The Senate confirmed Anna St. John as United States District Judge for the Eastern District of Louisiana and appointed General John M. "Jack" Keane to the Commission on the National Defense Strategy. With multiple committees meeting throughout the day to examine topics ranging from water and power legislation to worldwide threats, both chambers maintained active legislative schedules while preparing for continued debate on pending measures. The House is scheduled to consider three bills on March 18 including the Protecting Access for Hunters and Anglers Act, the Deporting Fraudsters Act of 2026, and legislation addressing harm to law enforcement animals, while the Senate will resume consideration of the House message accompanying the SAVE America Act at noon.

The Senate engaged in extended debate on the SAVE America Act, legislation designed to require proof of citizenship for voter registration. Senator Mike Lee of Utah, the bill's primary sponsor, argued that the measure represents a straightforward principle—that only American citizens should vote in American elections—and emphasized that 83 percent of Americans support voter ID requirements. Lee contended that the bill makes voting easy while making cheating hard, comparing citizenship verification to standard identification requirements in other contexts like banking and air travel. He addressed Democratic concerns about voter suppression by noting that the bill allows multiple forms of documentation and includes provisions for those without documents to submit sworn affidavits, placing the burden on states to verify or refute the information.

Senator Adam Schiff of California delivered an extensive critique, characterizing the SAVE America Act as a voter suppression effort designed to disenfranchise millions of eligible Americans rather than prevent fraud. Schiff argued that the bill would eliminate mail-in voting, online voter registration, and registration at the DMV, forcing Americans to travel to county clerk offices to register. He highlighted constituent letters expressing anxiety about inflation, healthcare costs, and economic hardship, asserting that instead of addressing these challenges, Republicans were attempting to restrict voting rights because they anticipated electoral losses. Schiff also criticized the administration's war with Iran and various policy failures, contending that the bill represents a cynical political maneuver to suppress voter turnout when the President's policies are unpopular.

Senator Wyden submitted an amendment establishing comprehensive cybersecurity protections for Electronic Benefit Transfer (EBT) cards used in the Supplemental Nutrition Assistance Program, requiring states to issue chip-enabled cards within two years and phasing out magnetic stripe cards within five years. The amendment mandates multiple user interfaces for account management, real-time transaction notifications, fraud reporting mechanisms, and enhanced security measures for online transactions, while requiring the Department of Agriculture to reimburse states for upgrade costs and maintain data on theft trends. Additional provisions protect households from losing access to benefits due to card damage or fraud, prohibit state agencies from charging replacement fees in specified circumstances, and require retailers accepting SNAP benefits to use chip-compatible payment terminals.

Several other amendments address distinct policy areas: Senator Blunt Rochester's proposal would extend enhanced premium tax credits for health insurance through 2028 and establish protections for Affordable Care Act enrollees; Senator Wyden's amendments would expand a school lunch procurement program for domestically grown produce, establish an office investigating agricultural competition violations, promote agritourism development, and fund coastal water quality monitoring; Senator Murphy's proposals would require citizenship documentation for firearms and machinegun purchases; Senator Gallego's amendment expresses the sense that constitutional protections prohibit immigration enforcement based on race or ethnicity; and Senator Whitehouse's amendment would prohibit federal settlements for individuals convicted of assaulting law enforcement during the January 6, 2021 Capitol breach.
