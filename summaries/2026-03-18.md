# Congressional Summary - 2026-03-18

[https://www.congress.gov/congressional-record/volume-172/issue-48/daily-digest](https://www.congress.gov/congressional-record/volume-172/issue-48/daily-digest)

On March 17, 2026, the Senate confirmed Anna St. John of Louisiana as United States District Judge for the Eastern District of Louisiana by a vote of 51-45, following a 48-45 motion to close debate on the nomination. The Senate continued consideration of the House message accompanying S. 1383, the Save America Act, with multiple pending amendments related to protecting women and girls in athletics and other provisions, with further consideration scheduled for March 18 at noon. Meanwhile, the House passed two suspension bills: H.R. 4294, the Mitigation Action and Watermen Support Act directing the Commerce Secretary to establish a pilot program for blue catfish sales in the Chesapeake Bay Watershed (320-66), and S. 3971, the Small Business Innovation and Economic Security Act extending the SBIR and STTR programs (345-41). The House also adopted H. Res. 1115 (196-193) providing for consideration of three bills: H.R. 556 on hunting and fishing access, H.R. 1958 on deportation of aliens who defraud the government, and H.R. 4638 on deportation of those who harm law enforcement animals, along with extended debate time for a constitutional amendment requiring a balanced federal budget.

Section 2 of this Congressional Record digest covers proceedings from March 17, 2026, including several one-minute speeches and extended remarks from House members, followed by Senate activity focused on the SAVE America Act regarding voter eligibility requirements. House members honored various constituents and organizations, including recognition of MyHometownBronxville news outlet, City Rescue Mission in Jacksonville, and Green Berets, while also addressing policy matters such as wealth inequality, hunger relief programs, and a Department of Homeland Security shutdown affecting TSA officers and federal employees. Senator Schiff delivered an extended critique of the SAVE America Act and the administration's Iran war policy, arguing that voting restrictions represent a political response to unpopular policies, while Senators Lee and Cantwell engaged in substantive debate over the bill's provisions requiring proof of citizenship for voter registration, with Lee defending the measure as necessary election security and Cantwell warning it would disenfranchise millions of eligible voters. The record also documents various Senate amendments proposed on topics ranging from childhood medical interventions to vaccine access to January 6 Capitol riot compensation restrictions.

Senate Amendment 4447, submitted by Ms. Blunt Rochester, proposes comprehensive changes to the Affordable Care Act focused on consumer protections, health plan accountability, and market stabilization. The amendment extends enhanced premium tax credits through 2028 instead of expiring in 2026, establishes an additional open enrollment period for plan year 2026, and restores funding for the Navigator program to help consumers understand their health insurance options. Additionally, the amendment requires health insurance issuers to notify enrollees about eligibility changes and enrollment opportunities, with enforcement mechanisms including civil penalties for non-compliance. The proposal addresses affordability by capping premium increases for consumers earning less than 400 percent of the poverty line, preventing what it terms "surprise premium bills," and restricting premium growth rates to historical levels.

The amendment also includes provisions to streamline administrative requirements, such as allowing automatic reenrollment from bronze to silver level plans and establishing special enrollment periods for low-income individuals experiencing income fluctuations. Market stabilization measures prevent insurers from increasing actuarial value variations and protect coverage continuity by requiring two consecutive years of non-compliance before removing individuals from advance premium tax credits. These provisions collectively aim to maintain insurance affordability and accessibility while reducing administrative burdens on consumers and promoting competition among health plans.

Section 4 of this 10-section Congressional Record digest presents seven separate Senate amendments addressing diverse policy areas, all submitted to S. 1383 (Veterans Advisory Committee on Equal Access) but ordered to lie on the table.

The amendments cover voting access, privacy protections, surveillance technology, firearms restrictions, and tax policy. Amendment 4417 (the Vote at Home Act of 2026) would establish automatic mail-in voting nationwide, requiring states to mail ballots to all registered voters at least two weeks before federal elections and providing free postage for ballot returns, with implementation beginning in 2028. Amendment 4416 prohibits federal funds from implementing Executive Order 14243 regarding information sharing and bans federal contracts with Palantir Technologies for processing personally identifiable information, with immediate effect. Amendment 4415 (Privacy Act Modernization Act of 2026) substantially expands privacy protections under the Privacy Act by broadening definitions of personal information and records, strengthening protections against government data collection and use, increasing civil remedies and criminal penalties for violations, and establishing immediate compliance requirements for entities related to the Department of Government Efficiency while providing a two-year implementation timeline for other agencies. Amendment 4414 (Cell-Site Simulator Warrant Act of 2026) comprehensively restricts law enforcement and intelligence community use of cell-site simulators, generally prohibiting their use except under specific warrant requirements or narrow exceptions, with detailed procedures for judicial oversight and mandatory disclosures regarding potential disruptions to emergency communications and public safety, effective two years after enactment. Amendment 4413 requires documentary proof of U.S. citizenship for assault weapon purchases under federal law. Amendments 4412, 4411, and 4410 address tax policy: Amendment 4412 eliminates tax benefits for outsourcing through modifications to international tax rules; Amendment 4411 increases payroll taxes and investment income taxes on high earners to strengthen Social Security and Medicare; and Amendment 4410 imposes a windfall profits tax on crude oil exceeding baseline prices and establishes gasoline rebates for eligible individuals funded by those tax revenues.

Senator Whitehouse's amendment proposes the Supreme Court Ethics, Recusal, and Transparency Act of 2026, which establishes comprehensive ethics standards and transparency requirements for the Supreme Court and federal judiciary. The amendment would require the Supreme Court to adopt a formal code of conduct within 180 days and make all ethics rules publicly available in searchable, downloadable formats on its website. Additionally, it creates new procedures for individuals to file complaints against justices for ethics violations or misconduct that undermines judicial integrity, with complaints reviewed by a five-judge investigation panel composed of circuit court chief judges who would publish findings and recommendations.

The amendment further strengthens disqualification requirements by expanding circumstances requiring justices and judges to recuse themselves, including situations involving gifts or financial benefits from parties to cases or their affiliates within six years prior, as well as cases where parties have made lobbying contacts or spent substantial funds supporting the judge's nomination. It establishes a review process for contested disqualification motions handled by randomly selected three-judge panels, requires parties and amici to disclose financial relationships with justices, mandates disclosure of funders contributing to amicus briefs, and directs the Federal Judicial Center to conduct biennial studies on compliance with recusal requirements, with reports to Congress and potential Government Accountability Office reviews.

Senator Whitehouse submitted four amendments addressing federal taxation and corporate accountability. The first proposes a Big Oil Windfall Profits Tax that would impose an excise tax on crude oil when prices exceed 2025 baseline levels, with revenues directed to a consumer relief fund providing rebate credits to eligible individuals through the tax system. The second amendment, the Medicare and Social Security Fair Share Act, would raise payroll taxes on high earners by raising the Social Security wage base to $400,000 and imposing a 1.2 percent additional Medicare tax on wages exceeding $400,000 for single filers and $500,000 for joint filers, with revenues allocated to Social Security and Medicare trust funds.

The third amendment, the No Tax Breaks for Outsourcing Act, implements multiple provisions to increase taxation of foreign corporate income and limit tax avoidance strategies. It would apply foreign tax credit limitations on a country-by-country basis for controlled foreign corporations, establish limits on interest deductions for domestic corporations in international financial reporting groups, repeal reduced tax rates for foreign-derived intangible income, increase deemed paid credits for foreign taxes on certain income, and strengthen rules against corporate inversions. The fourth amendment, submitted by Senator Murphy, would require documentary proof of United States citizenship for assault weapon purchases as defined under the National Voter Registration Act.

Section 7 of the Congressional Record digest addresses proposed amendments to S. 1383 that cover voting requirements, election administration, and related policy areas. The primary amendment (SA 4420) proposes comprehensive changes to federal voting regulations, including requirements for documentary proof of U.S. citizenship for voter registration and ballot casting, implementation of photo identification requirements for in-person and mail voting, restrictions on absentee voting to specific circumstances with documented justifications, and detailed ballot handling procedures with chain-of-custody requirements. The amendment establishes new coordination mechanisms between state election officials and federal agencies including the Department of Homeland Security and Social Security Administration to verify citizenship status, with states required to submit voter rolls to DHS for comparison through the Systematic Alien Verification for Entitlements system.

The section also includes numerous secondary amendments addressing related topics, including competitive amendments on sports eligibility and medical procedures for minors, amendments concerning firearm sales requirements, and various amendments from different senators addressing unrelated matters such as Russian sanctions, tariffs, labor standards, price discrimination, and healthcare policy. Several amendments focus on protecting individuals involved in January 6, preventing settlements or compensation to certain parties, and affirming vaccine safety and accessibility. The amendments range from those directly related to the underlying bill's election provisions to those using the legislative vehicle to advance various policy priorities on healthcare, agriculture, labor, and other federal concerns.

Senator Cortez Masto submitted an amendment to S. 3183, the wildland firefighter safety bill, that would redirect federal funding from Immigration and Customs Enforcement to the Community Oriented Policing Services (COPS) Hiring Program. The amendment modifies reconciliation legislation to extend the deadline for COPS grants from September 2029 to September 2030 and waives certain matching requirements for jurisdictions employing fewer than 175 law enforcement officers, including tribal governments. Additionally, it strikes provisions for immigration enforcement funding and instead appropriates $45 billion to the Attorney General for the Edward Byrne Memorial Justice Assistance Grant Program through fiscal year 2029.

The broader legislative activity in this section reflects continued Senate work on various bills and resolutions. Multiple resolutions were submitted recognizing significant observances and honoring individuals, including designations for Black Midwives Day, the International Year of Rangelands and Pastoralists, and National Osceola Turkey Day, as well as tributes to fallen service members. Numerous new bills were introduced covering topics ranging from healthcare workforce development and stem cell research reauthorization to tax provisions and consumer protection regulations, with many receiving bipartisan support and advancing through committee referral.

The Senate section focuses on the SAFEGUARD AMERICAN VOTER ELIGIBILITY ACT (SAVE America Act), legislation requiring proof of citizenship and photo identification to vote in federal elections. Senator Thune moved to proceed on S. 1383 with an amendment in a 51-48 vote, with Republicans generally supporting and Democrats opposing the measure. The legislative activity included multiple amendments addressing voter registration requirements, voter ID provisions, and restrictions on mail-in voting, alongside procedural motions regarding implementation timelines. Amendments also incorporated provisions protecting women's sports by defining sex based on reproductive biology at birth and addressing medical interventions for minors.

Democratic senators argued the bill constitutes voter suppression that would disenfranchise millions of eligible Americans, including married women whose names differ from birth certificates, military voters, rural residents, and those lacking passports or birth certificates. They contended the bill creates unnecessary burdens through in-person registration requirements, expensive documentation fees, and state-level voter roll purges coordinated through the Department of Homeland Security. Republican senators countered that basic voter identification and citizenship verification are common-sense measures supported by significant majorities of Americans across party lines, necessary to ensure election integrity and maintain voter confidence. Debate also touched on broader issues including the ongoing government shutdown at the Department of Homeland Security, immigration enforcement, and counterterrorism concerns. The section concluded with tributes to three Ohio National Guard members killed in a KC-135 aircraft crash in Iraq and discussion of a separate judicial nomination.

The Senate devoted this session primarily to debating the SAVE America Act, legislation requiring proof of citizenship for voter registration and photo identification at polling places, alongside provisions protecting women's sports and restricting gender transition procedures for minors. Republicans argued the bill represents common-sense election integrity measures supported by over 80 percent of Americans, while Democrats characterized it as voter suppression designed to prevent noncitizens from voting—a practice they contended was already illegal. A cloture motion on the nomination of Anna St. John to be United States District Judge for the Eastern District of Louisiana passed 48-45, moving forward judicial consideration. Additionally, Senator Ricketts reported on unprecedented wildfires in Nebraska that have burned over 750,000 acres, killed at least one person, and displaced thousands, while Senator Thune discussed the ongoing Department of Homeland Security shutdown, now in its 32nd day, noting its impact on TSA workers and national security amid heightened threats including Iran-linked cyber attacks and ISIS-inspired activities.
