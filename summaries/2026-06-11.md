# Congressional Summary (run on 2026-06-11)

[https://www.congress.gov/congressional-record/volume-172/issue-97/daily-digest](https://www.congress.gov/congressional-record/volume-172/issue-97/daily-digest)

On June 9, 2026, both chambers of Congress advanced legislative priorities with significant action on judicial nominations, immigration enforcement, and various policy measures. The Senate confirmed Anthony W. Mattivi as United States District Judge for the District of Kansas on a 51-46 vote and began consideration of additional judicial nominees, while advancing the Secure America Act reconciliation bill focused on immigration enforcement, which the House subsequently passed largely along party lines. Both chambers held numerous committee hearings on diverse topics including defense authorization, energy policy, labor relations, and financial crime, reflecting ongoing partisan divisions particularly regarding immigration funding and the use of reconciliation procedures to bypass traditional bipartisan appropriations processes.

The House passed H.R. 5408, the Faster Labor Contracts Act, which accelerates the timeline for negotiating initial collective bargaining agreements following union certification. The legislation requires employers to begin negotiations within ten days of certification and establishes a 90-day window for reaching agreements, with mandatory mediation and binding arbitration available if negotiations stall. Democrats supported the measure as preventing employer delay tactics that currently extend first contract negotiations to an average of 458 days, while Republicans opposed it as government overreach that removes workers' voting rights and empowers unelected bureaucrats over voluntary negotiations. The bill passed 230-193 with limited bipartisan support.

The House considered the Secure America Act, a comprehensive immigration enforcement bill allocating approximately $70 billion to Department of Homeland Security agencies. Republicans argued the legislation was necessary to prevent de facto defunding of immigration enforcement and cited statistics showing decreased southwest border apprehensions and increased fentanyl seizures, while emphasizing the bill funds normal operating budgets plus inflation over three years to prevent government shutdowns. Democrats opposed the measure as providing a blank check without accountability or guardrails, characterizing it as fiscally irresponsible and highlighting concerns about civil liberties violations and inadequate oversight. The bill ultimately passed 214-212 on largely party-line votes.

The Senate also addressed related matters including confirmation of the federal judiciary and advancement of various policy initiatives. Senators advanced discussions on election integrity, with some advocating increased use of the Department of Homeland Security's SAVE database to verify voter citizenship status, citing thousands of ineligible voters identified in participating states.
