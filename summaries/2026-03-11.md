# Daily Digest Summary - March 9, 2026

**Senate Activities**

The Senate convened at 3 p.m. for a busy session. Eight bills and five resolutions were introduced and referred to appropriate committees. The Senate focused significantly on the nomination of Lt. Gen. Joshua M. Rudd for Director of the National Security Agency, with substantial debate regarding his qualifications and constitutional knowledge. Senator Wyden expressed concerns about Rudd's responses on privacy rights and encryption policy. The Senate established a unanimous consent agreement setting a 12 noon Tuesday vote on Rudd's confirmation and a 2:15 p.m. vote on a cloture motion for Amendment No. 4308 to the Housing for the 21st Century Act. The Senate also received new nominations including Markwayne Mullin for Secretary of Homeland Security, replacing Kristi Noem.

**Senate Debate Topics**

Multiple senators addressed the Iran conflict, with Democratic leaders expressing concerns about military strategy and civilian casualties. Senator Schumer criticized the war's lack of clear objectives, while Senator Durbin objected to aggressive immigration enforcement tactics and defended due process rights. Senators emphasized concerns about gas prices spiking due to the conflict and questioned why Congress wasn't consulted before military operations began. Senator Grassley honored fallen military personnel at a dignified transfer ceremony at Dover Air Force Base, and bipartisan recognition was given to National Speech and Debate Education Day.

**Senate Committees**

Numerous committee meetings were scheduled throughout the week starting March 10. The Agriculture Committee would examine domestic agricultural consumption; the Armed Services Committee would hold closed briefings on Operation Epic Fury and review defense authorization requests; the Budget Committee would examine sanctuary cities and law enforcement; and the Commerce Committee would consider multiple nominations including those for NASA and transportation agencies.

**Department of Homeland Security Issues**

Democrats objected to a selective funding approach for DHS agencies, while Republicans emphasized border security gains under the Trump administration. The discussion centered on establishing reform measures for ICE and CBP officers, including body cameras, deescalation training, and professional standards similar to local law enforcement. Democrats criticized recent violent encounters involving immigration enforcement officers and advocated for proper procedures and oversight.
