# Congressional Summary (run on 2026-05-16)

[https://www.congress.gov/congressional-record/volume-172/issue-82/daily-digest](https://www.congress.gov/congressional-record/volume-172/issue-82/daily-digest)

On May 14, 2026, Congress engaged in substantial legislative activity across both chambers, with the Senate advancing nominations and passing measures on Capitol Police retirement age and senator pay during shutdowns, while the House completed work on three criminal justice bills and continued debate on the Military Construction and Veterans Affairs Appropriations Act. Congressional committees held extensive hearings on defense and education matters, examining fiscal year 2027 budget requests for military branches and various policy priorities, with the Senate Judiciary Committee favorably reporting multiple nominations and the House processing over 30 measures through committee markups.

Representative Schweikert of Arizona delivered a detailed analysis of the nation's fiscal crisis, highlighting that the United States borrows approximately $87,000 per second and that federal spending on entitlements—Social Security, Medicare, Medicaid, and interest payments—consumes all tax receipts, leaving defense and other government functions entirely dependent on borrowed funds. He warned that within 29 months, over half of federal spending will support those aged 65 and older, while Social Security and Medicare trust funds face depletion in approximately 6 years without reform, emphasizing that meaningful solutions exist but require political courage and honest conversation rather than easy rhetoric.

The House approved the Military Construction and Veterans Affairs Appropriations Act for fiscal year 2027, allocating $157 billion with strong bipartisan support for veterans' healthcare, a $50 billion Toxic Exposures Fund, military construction projects, and mental health investments. The bill achieved unanimous committee approval and passed with numerous amendments addressing military installation resilience, childcare facilities, medical research, and allowing VA healthcare providers to discuss state-approved cannabis programs with veterans.

The House debated three criminal justice bills with significant partisan disagreement: the Cashless Bail Reporting Act passed with bipartisan support despite Democratic concerns, while the Keeping Violent Offenders Off Our Streets Act and Monitor Accountability Act drew sharp criticism from Democrats who argued they would harm nonprofit bail assistance organizations and unfairly target specific jurisdictions. The Senate conducted routine business recognizing National Police Week and other observances, introduced legislation to expand study abroad opportunities and support various policy initiatives, and approved measures increasing Capitol Police retirement age and withholding senator pay during government shutdowns.
