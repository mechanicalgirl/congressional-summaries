# Congressional Summary (run on 2026-06-04)

[https://www.congress.gov/congressional-record/volume-172/issue-93/daily-digest](https://www.congress.gov/congressional-record/volume-172/issue-93/daily-digest)

The Senate met on June 2, 2026, to confirm two federal district court judges by narrow 52-46 margins: Kathleen S. Lane of Montana and Jeffrey M. Kuhlman of Kansas. The Senate also reached a unanimous consent agreement to vote on June 3 regarding Senate Joint Resolution 188, which would provide congressional disapproval of Environmental Protection Agency regulations on hazardous air pollutant emission standards for electric utility steam generating units. Nineteen bills and two resolutions were introduced before the Senate adjourned at 6:31 p.m.

The House convened the same day and passed eleven bills under suspension of the rules, addressing land transfers to Native American tribes, energy development on public lands, geothermal energy permitting, wildfire mitigation, rural water infrastructure, and fisheries management. Four additional bills were reported to committees covering charter school facilities, wildfire studies, marine mammal protection, and rural water systems. Thirty-two new bills and three resolutions were introduced, with numerous committee meetings scheduled for June 3 on budget requests, college sports oversight, health procedures, citizenship protections, and federal benefits programs.

The House advanced significant energy legislation including the bipartisan Geothermal Energy Advancement Act, which combines six bills to accelerate geothermal development on federal lands by establishing a Geothermal Ombudsman, streamlining permitting with specific deadlines, authorizing cost recovery fees, and updating royalty structures. The Committing Leases for Energy Access Now Act mandates annual geothermal lease sales and requires Interior to issue drilling permits within specified timeframes after environmental reviews. The License to Drill Act reauthorizes the Permit Processing Improvement Fund through 2037, allowing the Bureau of Land Management to collect application fees for oil and gas drilling permits.

The House also passed measures addressing tribal sovereignty and natural resources management, including the Albuquerque Indian School Act transferring approximately 9.89 acres to New Mexico's 19 pueblos, the Crystal Reservoir Conveyance Act conveying water infrastructure to Ouray Colorado, and the Unrecognized Southeast Alaska Native Communities Recognition and Compensation Act establishing urban corporations for five Alaska Native villages with approximately 23,040 acres of Tongass National Forest land for each.
